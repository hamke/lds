Coffee Drinkers Enjoy the Perks and Health Benefits of Grain-Based Beverages

August 6, 2014 / New York Daily News

Here’s a beverage that really goes against the grain.

Bottled oats and seedy lattes are sprouting all over — some blended into sweet coffees, others throwbacks to instant breakfast drinks — and New Yorkers are starting to slurp them up.

“It’s like cereal on the go,” says Freudna Mathurin, manager of Caffe Bene, a Korean-based chain in Midtown that boasts a five-grain coffee latte made with sesame, barley, black beans, brown rice and soybeans. “When I tell people what’s in it they’re repulsed, but when I whip it up, they are pleasantly surprised.”

It may sound hard to swallow, but drinks like this are delicious and nutritious, packed with omega-3s, fatty acids, protein and fiber. There’s also a quinoa smoothie, a Tahitian vanilla oat shake and even a juice made from chia seeds on the market.

“Quinoa is an amazing superfood,” says Gabriel Poblet Maristany, co-founder, who created the quinoa smoothie when he was bored with eating snack bars. “A drinkable snack is easier for your body to process.”

Caffe Bene’s concept is new to New Yorkers, but South Koreans have been sipping their grains for centuries. They call it misugaru — a protein-packed powder traditionally made with white and brown rice, barley, black rice, alda beans, black beans, corn, white bean, millet and sesame seeds that are ground, roasted and steamed and mixed together.

Mix it with coffee and milk and it’s a nutty healthy concoction. Add bananas, cinnamon and nut milks and you have yourself a smoothie.

“It’s a great way to feel full without consuming excess calories,” health expert and nutritionist Lisa Young tells The News. “You get vitamins and minerals like calcium, magnesium, folate and selenium.”

It’s not the first time breakfast has been served as a beverage. In the 1960s, Carnation took powdered protein, sugar and minerals and called it Instant Breakfast. In its current incarnation, that on-the-go meal has 220 calories and hefty doses of vitamins and minerals — but also 20 grams of sugar.

Almond and other nut milks have been around longer, as has Postum, a coffee substitute made with roasted, powdered wheat and wheat bran, that dates back to 1895.

What’s next? The future is certainly grainy.

“Wheat berries are the new farro,” celebrity chef Bobby Flay told The News.
