South Korean Coffeehouse Caffebene Opens Gardena Location

September 12, 2014 / Los Angeles Daily News

Korean coffee chain Caffebene is gunning for Starbucks’ market share by promising a warmer vibe, tastier sweets and a richer cup of joe.

The chain, which has grown to more than 1,400 shops worldwide since its 2009 founding, has opened a new store in Gardena.

The South Bay location is Caffebene’s third in the Los Angeles area. The company has plans to open another seven in the region by the end of the year.

Caffebene’s staples are gourmet coffee drinks and desserts geared towards the European sweet tooth, such as Belgian waffles, gelato and honey bread, a topping-heavy version of French toast. Pastries, sandwiches, quiches and fresh baked breads are also on the menu.

Inspired by European cafe culture, Caffebene offers a relaxed atmosphere and tempting dessert menu that encourages customers to spend time at the store.

“It’s the antithesis of Starbucks or Dunkin’ Donuts, which just serves you coffee and that’s it,” said Caffebene’s director of franchise development, John Barry. “It’s the respect — the hospitality, honoring the guest — that just doesn’t exist in the retail coffee market.”

The formula seems to be working in South Korea, where Caffebene enjoys the same brand recognition as Starbucks. The chain’s popularity in Korea is what drove Edward Yoon to open the Gardena store.

“It looks good, right?” Yoon said as he wrapped up renovations to the building at 1542 S. Western Ave., which he also owns.

He pointed to the sitting area with bright, forest-themed wallpaper, several wall-mounted book shelves and a generous supply of electrical outlets for students to plug in their computers.

“The invoice is higher because [customers] order coffee with something else,” Yoon said. “They can stay here all day!”

Kim Sun-Kwon founded Caffebene in 2009 in Seoul, South Korea. The first U.S. store opened in Times Square in early 2013, with the first Los Angeles location opening in August 2013.

Another 86 shops are expected to open in the U.S. by the end of the year, and the company hopes to open 8,000 to 10,000 more stores worldwide by 2020.

Creative marketing has fueled Caffebene’s explosive expansion in Asia. Founder Kim Sun-Kwon entered into deals with the country’s wildly popular soap operas to film episodes inside Caffebene stores and arranged for celebrities to be photographed hanging out at its locations.

“The president is very well-connected with the Hollywood people in Korea,” Barry said.

Brand integration with Korean soap operas is nothing new, according to Asian American Advertising Federation Vice President Jay Kim, who is based in Los Angeles.

Soap operas create official partnerships with doughnut shops, restaurants and hospitals, Kim said. They are even starting to market Korean brand products in countries like China, where Korean soap operas are wildly popular.

“With K-pop and K-soaps, people tend to think a lot of Korean things are cool,” Kim said. “It’s like way way back with Hollywood, which made everything American cool.”

The large number of multi-generational family homes in Korea has also contributed to Caffebene’s success, Kim said. Children tend to live with their parents longer than in America, which means kids tend to gravitate toward cafes and other public spaces.

“They are looking for cool places to hang out,” Kim said. “Starbucks does so much product display, it’s almost difficult to get in and out. Caffebene is easier to come and hang out. It’s got a better, edgier design.”

After renovations and buying Caffebene’s ovens and coffee machines, it costs about $450,000 to open a franchise, plus a 4.5 to 5.5 percent monthly advertising fee, Yoon said.

For Junghyun Suh, a building contractor who opened the second Caffebene in Los Angeles in March 2013, it was much cheaper because he designed and built his own shop.

Business started off a little slow, but the clientele has really picked up in 2014, Suh said. He attributes his shop’s success to the modern-industrial decor and high ceilings and to Caffebene’s “exclusive menu.”

Suh said he was inspired to open his own shop after he was hired to do the contracting work on L.A.’s first Caffebene, which had opened six months earlier.
